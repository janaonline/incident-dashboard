The reform record — systemic change after disaster
—

incident out of 77 produced documented systemic reform

The New Delhi Railway Station stampede (2025) is the only incident in this dataset for which crowd-control reforms were subsequently announced. In the remaining incidents, the documented response ends at inquiry, compensation, or ongoing investigation. FIRs, inquiries, and compensation are incident-level responses — they are consistently triggered. Regulatory reform is a systems-level response — it is exceptional.

Key questions for policy and reform
These patterns point to questions for city administrators, regulators, and policymakers — framed as questions, not recommendations, because the appropriate response depends on institutional context.
1
Why does enforcement not follow inspection?
Inspection and enforcement failures co-occur across incident types. Are inspections conducted, are findings recorded, and do findings trigger action?
2
What explains BMC's concentration of incidents?
10 incidents in one municipal body, all at high preventability, over a decade. Does this reflect scale, documentation quality, or institutional design?
3
What happens to warnings?
Prior warnings were documented in roughly half of incidents. What processes exist for recording, escalating, and acting on risk signals — and where do they break?
4
What does the accountability process produce?
Zero convictions across a decade of fatal incidents raises questions about the gap between inquiry and adjudication.
5
Which regulatory functions are under-resourced?
Inspection and enforcement failures span fire safety, industrial safety, building standards, and crowd management — suggesting capacity constraints that cut across departments.
6
What conditions produced the one reform?
Understanding what made reform possible in the New Delhi Railway Station case — and absent in the other 76 — is directly relevant to designing better accountability mechanisms.

Data: 77 incidents compiled from news reports, Wikipedia, government inquiry records, court records, CAG reports, and credible media investigations (2016–2026). This dashboard renders live from a Google Sheet — the spreadsheet is the single source of truth. Source links for every incident are shown in its expanded card.

Methodology notes: Some incidents appear as near-duplicate entries reflecting multiple source reports of the same event — all rows are retained as documented in the source dataset. Map coordinates are city-level approximations geocoded from location names, not precise incident-site coordinates. The governance failure prevalence chart (Section "Governance failure patterns") reflects published aggregate findings from the original 26-category coding and is shown as a standalone reference, not re-derived from this sheet.
